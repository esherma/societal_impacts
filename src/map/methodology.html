Methodology & Sources

How the AI Exposure Index is constructed, what it measures, and what it does not.

What the Index Measures

The AI Exposure Index answers: How concentrated is this county's workforce in occupations where AI systems are currently being actively used? It is a weighted average of occupation-level AI activity scores, where the weights are each occupation's share of the local civilian workforce.

The index is not a prediction of job loss. It measures current observed usage of AI tools (specifically, Claude) across occupation types — not theoretical capability, not future automation potential. Counties with high scores have large shares of workers in occupations where AI is already integrated into daily work. Counties with low scores have workforces concentrated in occupations where AI usage has not yet been observed at scale.

The practical scale: county index values in the US range from roughly 0.035 (rural agricultural counties, Appalachia) to 0.150 (Northern Virginia tech corridor, Silicon Valley). The national county mean is approximately 0.087.

Construction Method: Bartik Shift-Share

The core method is a Bartik-style shift-share, the same approach used in the China shock literature (Autor, Dorn & Hanson 2013) for constructing geographic exposure to national-level labor market shocks.

AI_Exposure(county) = Σk [ sharek(county) × exposure_scorek ]

Where:

  • sharek(county) is the fraction of the county's civilian employed population in occupation group k
  • exposure_scorek is the mean observed AI exposure across all 6-digit SOC occupations within ACS occupation group k

The shift-share approach attributes variation in county-level exposure entirely to the county's occupational mix — not to local adoption patterns or industry structure. This is both a strength (it's purely driven by workforce composition, which is well-measured) and a limitation (two counties with identical occupation mixes get the same score even if one has much higher AI adoption).

Known Limitations

Aggregation to broad occupation groups

The ACS summary tables report employment at 25 broad occupation groups (e.g., "Computer and mathematical occupations"), not at 6-digit SOC detail. Within each group, the category score is an unweighted mean across all matching SOC occupations — which masks substantial within-group variation. For example, "Computer programmers" (74.5% exposure) and "Network support specialists" (3.2% exposure) are both in the "Computer and mathematical" group, averaged to a group score of 37.9%.

Small-county sampling noise

ACS 5-year estimates for counties with fewer than ~5,000 workers carry large margins of error. Occasionally, random sampling fluctuations produce anomalous occupation concentrations. Dolores County, CO (1,336 workers) is a known example where the estimate implies an unusually high share of computer/math workers. Exercise caution interpreting outliers in very small counties.

Geographic unit mismatch with labor markets

Counties are administrative units that often don't correspond to actual labor market areas. San Bernardino County, CA has a land area larger than nine US states and contains dramatically different labor markets (coastal suburbs, inland logistics corridors, desert communities). A single county-level index obscures this heterogeneity. Commuting zone and PUMA-level analysis are planned.

Observed ≠ affected

High AI Exposure Index does not mean workers in those counties are at risk of displacement. The Anthropic data measures where Claude is being used — in many cases for augmentation (AI assisting workers) rather than automation (AI replacing tasks). The index is a measure of current AI activity in the occupational mix, not a prediction of labor market outcomes.

Planned Features Phase 2

  • Commuting zone resolution. Aggregate county data to BEA/USDA commuting zones (~709 areas), which better reflect actual labor market geography. Will require building commuting zone TopoJSON boundaries from county polygons.
  • Augmentation vs. automation split. The Anthropic Economic Index distinguishes task-level augmentation (AI assisting) from automation (AI replacing). A second map layer showing automation-heavy vs. augmentation-heavy regions would make the policy implications clearer.
  • Theoretical exposure toggle. Current map shows "observed" exposure (where Claude is actually being used). A second layer using Eloundou et al. (2023) theoretical β scores would show where AI could be deployed based on capability alone — the gap between observed and theoretical is a measure of latent risk.
  • Employment-weighted category scores. Current category scores use unweighted occupation averages. Weighting by national employment within each group (from BLS OES data) would give more accurate category scores.
  • PUMA-level microdata. ACS Public Use Microdata (PUMS) with 6-digit SOC codes would allow more precise matching to the Anthropic occupation scores, at the cost of PUMA-level geography instead of county-level.
